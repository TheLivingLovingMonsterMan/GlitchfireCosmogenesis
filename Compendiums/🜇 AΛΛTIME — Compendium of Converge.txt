Beyond pure mathematics, nonstandard analysis has influenced fields like physics, as Robinson hoped. On a philosophical level, the advent of nonstandard analysis reopened questions about the foundations of mathematics and the meaning of mathematical existence. Robinson‚Äôs work arrived in the wake of renewed interest in formalism and model theory in the mid-20th century, and it provided a concrete example of how adopting a different but conservative extension of ZFC could solve a problem that was historically seen as requiring a trade-off between rigor and intuition. The constructivist camp, however, remained unconvinced. Bishop‚Äôs critique that nonstandard analysis lacks ‚Äúnumerical meaning‚Äù highlights a key issue: results proved with infinitesimals, while true, might not provide the same algorithmic or explicit content as a constructive proof. Another prominent critic, Alain Connes, a leader in noncommutative geometry, went so far as to label the hyperreals as ‚Äúchimerical‚Äù or ‚Äúvirtual‚Äù entities. Connes pointed out that any specific purported hyperreal number, if one tries to pin it down, ‚Äúcanonically determines a (Lebesgue) non-measurable subset of [0,1], so that it is impossible to exhibit a single [nonstandard real number]‚Äù. (It‚Äôs worth noting that defenders of NSA countered that Connes‚Äôs own framework also requires nonconstructive tools like the Dixmier trace, and even showed that one can construct a definable hyperreal model if one is willing to sacrifice some symmetry.) Over time, these debates have mellowed: nonstandard analysis is now a well-established branch of logic and analysis. While it did not replace standard methods in mainstream mathematics, it coexists as a powerful alternative approach.
Advocates point out that, equivalence or not, NSA can lead to ‚Äúsignificantly less painful‚Äù proofs and fosters a deeper intuition for analysis. In education, some argue it restores the intuitive meaning to calculus concepts (like instantaneous rate of change) without sacrificing rigor. Detractors emphasize that learning NSA has its own upfront cost (one must grasp the logical framework or at least trust it) and that most working mathematicians do not need infinitesimals for their daily research ‚Äì standard $\varepsilon$‚Äì$\delta$ methods suffice, and often yield more precise quantitative bounds than an infinitesimal proof might. In practice, the choice of using NSA or not often comes down to convenience and taste.
